On Tuesday I argued Google is turning from a traffic engine into an answer
engine — ads are moving *inside* AI Overviews and AI Mode, and landing pages
should be judged by qualified-lead rate and revenue per visitor, not traffic.

A few of you replied, reasonably: "Cool theory. What does it do to my numbers?"

So I built the project. Here's the payoff.

I simulated a year of paid search (synthetic data, modeled on the public
research — Pew's ~15%→8% CTR drop when an AI summary shows up, Similarweb's
zero-click climb from ~56% to ~69%). Then I watched the dashboard the way a
nervous growth team would.

The alarm: total paid-search leads fell ~26%.

The instinct that follows is "cut paid search." That instinct is wrong — and
it's the expensive kind of wrong.

When I decomposed it:
→ The drop was ~60% informational queries. Commercial and transactional barely
moved.
→ Where an AI Overview was present, informational lead *yield* fell ~59%.
→ Meanwhile the qualified-lead rate ROSE ~30%, and revenue per visitor jumped
~70%.

Lead volume fell. Lead quality climbed. The issue was the **mix**, not the
performance.

Here's the reframe that changes the decision: **AI search isn't cutting your
leads — it's filtering them.**

Remember when AI Overviews confidently told people to put glue on their pizza
and eat a rock a day? AI is great at answering low-stakes informational
questions — which is exactly the cheap, low-intent traffic that was never
becoming pipeline anyway. It's quietly running a filter on the top of your
funnel, and mostly catching the clicks that never converted.

The move isn't to cut. It's to reallocate: budget toward commercial intent,
landing pages rebuilt around proof (comparisons, calculators, demos), and a
scoreboard that measures qualified-lead rate and revenue per visitor instead of
raw leads.

In the model, cutting the program forfeited ~$10M in annual revenue. Reallocating
the same budget added ~$1.3M. Same spend. Very different quarter.

If your paid-search leads are sliding, the question isn't "how do we get the
volume back?" It's: **are you losing leads, or just losing the leads that were
never going to buy?**

How is your team rethinking paid search as AI answers more of the top of the
funnel — cutting, holding, or reallocating?
